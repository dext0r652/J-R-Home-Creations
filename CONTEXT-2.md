## Design rubric (mandatory — every decision serves one of these)

### A. The Blink Test
> Does the site look professional **and human-made** within the first 3 seconds?

The internet is flooded with AI-generated websites — templated, glowing-gradient, generic-blue-on-white garbage. The competitive edge is to look human AND intentional. Visitors must feel they're dealing with real people running a real business — but the site should still **look designed**.

Two failure modes to avoid (not just one):

**Failure 1: AI aesthetic.** Glowing gradient hero, perfect symmetry everywhere, generic sans-serif + flat blue accent, stock photos of office-people smiling at laptops, motion-as-decoration. Reads as generated.

**Failure 2: Lazy minimalism.** Pure black-on-white, one accent color only on buttons, every section the same width and rhythm, no use of the brand palette captured in §5, no shapes or color blocks, no asymmetry. Reads as a wireframe shipped to production.

**Aim for: hyper performant AND visually appealing.** Use the brand palette confidently — primary AND accent, in shapes, backgrounds, callout blocks, not just on buttons. Modern layout patterns are welcome where they fit (bento grids for services or proof points, asymmetric hero compositions, deliberate negative space, overlapping cards). The goal is a site that looks like a designer thought about it, not a template.

**Concrete cues for "intentional design":**
- Real photos from §10 used at meaningful sizes
- Brand color used as a section background at least once (not just buttons)
- A layout decision the visitor will notice — bento grid, asymmetric hero, prominent service cards with their own color treatment
- Typography pair from §5 (not Inter + Roboto)
- Slight, deliberate asymmetry — not perfect symmetry

### B. The Elevator Pitch
> Can a stranger understand exactly what this business does in under 5 seconds from the homepage hero alone?

The hero answers all of: who's this for, what do they do, what's the next action. No vague taglines. No "Solutions". No running at the message from a distance.

### C. Speed Over Everything
> Mobile load ≤ 3s. No exceptions.

The goal is **hyper performant AND visually appealing** — not a tradeoff. With Next.js Image, server components, CSS transforms instead of motion libraries, and properly compressed images, a visually rich site that hits Lighthouse 90+ is the norm, not the exception.

- Lighthouse mobile performance ≥ 90
- LCP < 2.5s (target < 2s)
- All images optimized + lazy-loaded below the fold
- CSS-only animation; avoid `framer-motion` / `react-spring` at homepage scope
- No carousels, no parallax, no auto-play video

### D. The Four Visitor Questions

The homepage must answer all four:
1. **Am I in the right place?** — clear from the hero headline
2. **Do they get my problem?** — sub-line speaks to the situation
3. **What's their offer?** — services section, pricing where possible
4. **Can I trust them?** — trust ribbon + photos + reviews/longevity

### E. Reduce Social Friction

Local-services customers have strong preferences about how they reach a business. Tradespeople customers often call; younger demographics often WhatsApp; some email; some fill forms. **A site that offers only one path loses everyone who prefers the others.**

Build for that:

- **Phone in the header on every page,** as a `tel:` link, with a phone icon, mobile-tappable (≥44px tap target).
- **Multiple contact methods on the contact page, side-by-side, equally weighted.** Phone · WhatsApp · email · form — not "Contact us → here's only a form." Don't gate the phone number behind a form.
- **"Preferred contact method" selector on the contact form.** A small radio group: "How should I get back to you? Phone / Email / WhatsApp / Whichever's quickest." Signals you care AND gives the form better data.
- **WhatsApp floating action button** where the trade fits — trades, restaurants, salons, healthcare. Skip for law / accounting (reads informal).
- **Single hero CTA stays the rule.** Multiple CTAs in the hero splits attention. But the hero's single CTA can land on a contact page that exposes all the channels.

### F. Mandatory homepage layout (in this exact order)

1. **Hero.** Headline + sub + ONE CTA only. Hero photo from `/public/photos/` if available. Use the brand palette boldly here — this is where intentional design lands first.
2. **Trust ribbon.** Single horizontal band, ≤80px tall. 3–5 quick credibility cues from §6.
3. **Services section.** One card per service from §3. Card = name + 1-line description + "Learn more →" link. A bento-style grid (mixed card sizes) is welcome here when there are 4+ services.
4. **Optional social proof block.** ONE testimonial or rating widget. Only if real testimonials exist; else skip the section entirely.
5. **Final CTA.** Repeat the hero CTA, inverted color block (use the brand primary as the background).
6. **Footer.** Phone (tel:), email, address, hours, VAT/BTW, social links (Facebook / Instagram / Google Business if listed in §2), privacy/terms links, Novin Digital credit (see "Mandatory footer credit" below).

### Anti-patterns (do NOT include)

- Multiple competing CTAs on the homepage hero
- Vague taglines ("Solutions", "Empowering your business")
- Stock photos of grinning people in offices
- Hero carousels / sliders / parallax / auto-play video
- Glowing gradient backgrounds with no other identity
- Pure-monochrome design that ignores the brand palette from §5
- 8+ menu items in the nav (cap at 5)
- 60-link footer
- Cookie consent banner (skip unless explicitly requested)
- Testimonial walls when there are no real testimonials
- Phone or contact info gated behind a form

## Tech stack (mandatory)

- **Next.js 16.x** App Router with TypeScript, strict mode. **Important:** Next.js 16 has breaking changes vs. older versions. Read `node_modules/next/dist/docs/` BEFORE writing route handlers, generateMetadata, or middleware. Don't trust your training data on Next.js 16.
- **Tailwind 4** with CSS-first config (`@theme inline { ... }` in `app/globals.css`). PostCSS plugin: `@tailwindcss/postcss`.
- **shadcn/ui** (style: `new-york` or `radix-nova`, base color `neutral`, lucide icons). Set up via `components.json`.
- **Resend** for the contact form — but **leave the API key empty** in `.env.local.example`. The form should validate visually but the route handler returns `{ ok: true }` without actually sending. Add a `// HANDOFF` comment explaining this.
- **Vercel** for hosting.
- Server Components by default. Client components only where interactivity is required (form state, mobile menu toggle).
